# Business Case: Field Service Dispatch and Parts Readiness Modernization

**Prepared for:** COO and CFO  
**Prepared by:** Business Operations PMO  
**Date:** 2026-05-27  
**Version:** Fictional sample  
**Classification:** Public-safe synthetic example

## Executive Summary

Field service productivity is being reduced by a readiness gap between dispatch scheduling, parts availability, and technician route planning. Based on the fictional planning data supplied for this sample, 120 technicians lose an estimated 6.5 productive hours per technician per month, while 18% of work orders require emergency rescheduling and 14% are delayed by parts-readiness failures.

The recommended path is a phased dispatch and parts readiness modernization program. The program should begin with a 90-day readiness pilot across three service territories, then scale only after the organization validates the baseline, confirms integration complexity, and proves adoption by dispatch supervisors and field managers.

The estimated investment is $620,000 in one-time cost plus $180,000 in annual recurring cost. The hard benefit model estimates $1.25 million in annual benefit from recovered technician productivity and avoided reschedule premium. Under the base case, payback is approximately 7 months after launch. This is a planning estimate, not a finance-validated forecast.

The primary risk is that work-order, inventory, and scheduling data quality may be weaker than assumed. If the data cannot reliably support a readiness signal, the initiative would require additional process remediation before full rollout. The mitigation is to perform a four-week data and workflow validation before vendor configuration or enterprise rollout.

The decision requested is approval from the COO and CFO by June 30, 2026 to fund Phase 1 and authorize the 90-day pilot. Delaying the decision past that date would push benefit realization into the next planning cycle and prolong the current productivity leakage.

## 1. Problem Statement and Business Need

### Observable Symptoms

Dispatch assignments are often made before parts readiness and travel sequencing are confirmed. The result is avoidable technician downtime, rescheduled visits, supervisor rework, and inconsistent customer commitments.

### Root Cause Analysis

The working root-cause hypothesis is not simply that the current tools are inadequate. The deeper issue is that scheduling, inventory readiness, and route planning are governed as separate workflows. No single readiness signal exists before dispatch commitment.

## 5. Recommended Solution

### Recommendation

Approve Option 2: a phased readiness signal and dispatch workflow modernization program.

### Rationale

Option 2 addresses the root cause without forcing a full platform replacement. It creates a readiness gate, validates data quality, tests supervisor adoption, and allows the company to stop or redirect before enterprise rollout if assumptions fail.

### Solution Description

**People:** Dispatch supervisors, field managers, inventory coordinators, and IT operations owners will participate in readiness-rule design and pilot governance.

**Process:** Work orders must pass a parts-readiness and route-feasibility check before dispatch commitment, with defined override authority.

**Technology:** Existing scheduling and inventory systems will feed a readiness signal. The pilot should validate integration needs before broader automation.

### Dependencies and Prerequisites

- Work-order history and inventory status data must be accessible for baseline validation.
- Dispatch supervisors must participate in workflow design.
- IT must confirm integration feasibility before vendor configuration.
- Finance must validate loaded labor rate and benefit methodology.
