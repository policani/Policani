# Business Case Playbook

## Definition

A business case is the decision document that justifies why an initiative should be funded, started, changed, deferred, or rejected. It uses the problem, options, costs, benefits, risks, assumptions, constraints, and recommended path to support a leadership decision.

It sits before the project charter. The business case answers whether the idea is worth moving into formal initiation. The project charter starts the project after the decision has been made.

## When to build one

Build a business case when an idea needs approval, funding, prioritization, or governance sign-off.

It is especially useful when:

- There are multiple ways to solve the problem.
- The current state has a real cost of inaction.
- The initiative affects strategy, budget, people, technology, risk, compliance, customer experience, or the operating model.
- Leadership needs a decision record that can be used later in governance.

## Prerequisites

Before drafting, clarify:

- Business problem or opportunity
- Strategic objective
- Current-state pain
- Desired future state
- Key stakeholders
- Rough scope and timing
- Available evidence
- Cost, benefit, risk, and assumption inputs
- Known constraints
- Decision owner and approval path

Weak data does not prevent drafting, but it must be labeled honestly. A good business case can say, “This is the best current recommendation, but these assumptions must be validated before full funding.”

## Standard structure

1. Executive Summary
2. Decision Request
3. Problem or Opportunity Statement
4. Strategic Alignment
5. Current State and Gap Analysis
6. Options Analysis, including Do Nothing
7. Recommended Solution
8. Benefits and Value Case
9. Costs, Funding, and ROI Considerations
10. Risks, Assumptions, and Constraints
11. Implementation Approach, Milestones, and Ownership
12. Governance and Success Measures
13. Next Steps
14. Appendix: Evidence Summary

## What it feeds into

If approved, the business case typically feeds:

- Project initiation
- Project charter
- Funding approval
- Portfolio prioritization
- Discovery or requirements work
- Detailed project planning
- Governance checkpoints

It becomes the baseline for why the work exists. Later governance should test whether delivery is still aligned to the original value proposition.
