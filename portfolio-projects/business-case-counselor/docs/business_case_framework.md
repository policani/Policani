# Business Case Framework

A complete business case is a decision document. It should justify why an initiative should be funded, started, changed, deferred, or rejected using the problem, options, costs, benefits, risks, and recommended path.

## Required sections

1. **Executive Summary** — stands alone; includes problem, recommendation, cost, return, primary risk, and ask.
2. **Problem Statement & Business Need** — observable symptoms, root cause, status quo impact, scope, and urgency.
3. **Strategic Alignment** — explicit link to organizational priorities, not generic mission language.
4. **Current State Assessment** — baseline metrics, gaps, sources, and data limitations.
5. **Options Analysis** — Do Nothing plus real alternatives evaluated against consistent criteria.
6. **Recommended Solution** — named recommendation, rationale, people/process/technology impact, dependencies.
7. **Financial Analysis** — cost model, hard/soft benefits, ROI/payback, assumptions, sensitivity analysis.
8. **Risk Assessment** — named risks, likelihood, impact, severity, mitigation, residual risk, owner.
9. **Implementation Roadmap** — phases, milestones, resources, dependencies, critical path, 90-day wins.
10. **Governance & Decision Framework** — authority, escalation, metrics, review gates, steady-state ownership.
11. **Recommendation & Call to Action** — specific decision, decision-maker, deadline, consequence of delay, next steps.

## Options discipline

Do Nothing is always included, but it must not be treated as a strawman. Quantify what deteriorates if nothing changes.

When the real decision is a design choice, frame options around genuine design archetypes:

- Centralized control vs. lightweight overlay vs. federated model.
- Build vs. buy vs. configure.
- Restrict vs. govern vs. enable with guardrails.
- Full program vs. pilot vs. phased rollout.

When the user has already named a binary decision, do not force a fake third option. Add Do Nothing as Option 0 and evaluate the two real choices honestly.

## Financial discipline

Hard benefits belong in ROI. Soft benefits do not.

Examples of hard benefits:

- Labor efficiency converted to dollars.
- Revenue protection or uplift.
- Avoided cost.
- Compliance penalty avoidance with documented exposure.
- Cycle-time reduction translated to cost, revenue, or working-capital impact.

Examples of soft benefits:

- Better employee experience.
- Cleaner operating visibility.
- Improved collaboration.
- Stronger confidence in decisions.

Soft benefits can support the case, but they should not inflate the financial headline.

## Risk discipline

A risk is not a category. It is a named failure mode with consequence.

Weak: “Change management risk.”

Stronger: “Field supervisors may reject new dispatch discipline if exception handling adds administrative work during peak season, delaying adoption and reducing expected route-efficiency gains.”
