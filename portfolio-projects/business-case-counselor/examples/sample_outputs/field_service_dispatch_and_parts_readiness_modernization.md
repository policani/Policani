## 3. Problem or Opportunity Statement

Field service work orders are entering through email, spreadsheets, phone requests, and inconsistent ticket fields. The fragmented intake process causes dispatch rework, parts misses, overtime, and engineering interruptions. Leadership needs a decision on whether to fund a governed intake and parts-readiness modernization rather than continue absorbing operational leakage.

## 4. Strategic Alignment

The initiative supports service reliability, margin protection, customer responsiveness, field technician productivity, and better use of engineering capacity. It also creates a cleaner operating baseline for future automation without assuming automation value before process and data quality are improved.

## 5. Current State and Gap Analysis

### Current State

The service organization receives roughly 3,800 work orders per month across four regions. Intake quality varies by requester and channel. Dispatchers often reclassify work, planners manually check parts availability, and engineers are pulled into avoidable triage when symptoms, site configuration, or prior repair history are missing. Managers use weekly spreadsheet reconciliations to understand backlog, expedite costs, and missed appointments.

### Gap Analysis

The gap is not a single missing tool. The organization lacks a standard intake model, required work-order data fields, triage rules, parts-readiness checks, ownership for data quality, and a governance rhythm for backlog and exception review. Existing systems contain useful data, but the operating model does not convert that data into reliable dispatch decisions.

### Desired Future State

Field service requests enter through a governed intake path with required data fields, clear triage rules, automated or semi-automated parts-readiness checks, integrated visibility into dispatch status, and weekly governance over backlog, exceptions, field constraints, and benefits realization.

## 6. Options Analysis

| Option | Description | Advantages | Disadvantages | Decision Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Do nothing | Continue operating with current email, spreadsheet, and ticket-field practices. | No near-term funding required; No delivery disruption; No process change burden | Dispatch rework and overtime continue; Parts misses remain unmanaged; Engineering remains exposed to avoidable triage; Managers continue relying on lagging spreadsheet reconciliations | Valid baseline only; it does not address the cost of inaction. |
| Policy-only standardization | Publish standard intake rules and require regions to improve work-order fields without system or workflow changes. | Low cost; Can start quickly; Clarifies management expectations | Compliance likely weak without workflow enforcement; Limited visibility into exceptions; Benefits depend on manual behavior change; Parts-readiness gap remains largely unchanged | Useful as a supporting action, but insufficient as the primary solution. |
| Governed workflow modernization using existing platforms | Standardize intake, define triage rules, add parts-readiness checkpoints, integrate available system data, and pilot the model in two regions before scaling. | Addresses root operating-model gaps; Avoids premature platform replacement; Creates measurable pilot evidence; Improves confidence before full implementation funding | Requires cross-functional participation; Benefits depend on adoption and data-quality ownership; Some integration assumptions must be validated | Recommended path because it balances value, speed, and investment control. |
| Full field service platform replacement | Select and implement a new field service management platform across regions. | Could address broader capability gaps; May improve technician experience and future optimization options | Highest cost and delivery risk; Long procurement and implementation cycle; May automate poor data and process discipline; Not justified until the operating model and value case are validated | Potential future path, but not recommended for the current decision. |

## 7. Recommended Solution

Approve an 8-week discovery and pilot-design phase for governed workflow modernization using existing platforms where practical. The phase should validate baseline leakage, define the intake and dispatch operating model, design the parts-readiness workflow, assess integration effort, and produce a gated implementation request for a two-region pilot.

## 10. Risks, Assumptions, and Constraints

| Type | Item | Impact | Mitigation / Validation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Risk | Treating the problem as a tool-selection exercise | The organization could buy or configure technology without resolving intake quality, ownership, and dispatch governance. | Require operating-model design and data-quality ownership before implementation approval. |
| Risk | Weak regional adoption | Regions may continue using side spreadsheets or informal escalation channels, reducing measurable benefit. | Pilot with two regions, assign regional change owners, and review adoption metrics weekly. |
| Risk | Integration complexity underestimated | Costs and schedule could increase if service, ERP, and inventory data are harder to connect than expected. | Complete technical discovery and interface validation before implementation funding. |
| Risk | Parts data quality limits readiness checks | The workflow may flag exceptions inconsistently if parts master data or inventory timing is unreliable. | Add data profiling and exception taxonomy to discovery exit criteria. |
| Assumption | Current operational leakage is material enough to justify pilot investment. | Value case weakens if baseline leakage is lower than the dummy estimates. | Validate overtime, expedite freight, repeat visits, rework, and parts exceptions during discovery. |
| Assumption | Existing service and inventory platforms can support phase-1 integration. | Scope, cost, and timing may increase if custom integration is required. | Complete integration assessment and proof-of-connectivity during discovery. |
| Assumption | Two-region pilot results can reasonably inform scaling decision. | Scaling risk increases if pilot regions are not representative. | Select one high-volume and one moderate-volume region with different service profiles. |
| Constraint | Peak maintenance season begins in Q4. | Late implementation could collide with field capacity constraints. | Complete discovery before Q3 portfolio lock and time pilot launch before peak demand. |
| Constraint | Implementation funding is not yet approved. | The team cannot commit platform configuration or integration build beyond discovery. | Limit the immediate request to discovery/pilot design and a conditional funding range. |
| Constraint | Field technician time for workshops is limited. | Poor field input could weaken workflow design. | Use short, structured workshops and validate with ride-along or dispatcher shadowing notes. |
