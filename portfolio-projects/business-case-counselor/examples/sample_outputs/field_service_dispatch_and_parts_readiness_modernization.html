1. Problem Statement & Business Need

Observable Symptoms

  • First-time fix rate is estimated at 71% against an internal target of 82%.
  • Technicians report arriving without required parts on roughly 14% of service visits.
  • Dispatch supervisors manually rebalance schedules late in the day three to four times per week.

Root Cause Analysis

The working hypothesis is that the issue is not technician capacity alone; it is fragmented dispatch planning and weak parts-readiness governance before the appointment is committed.

Root-Cause Challenge

The common diagnosis is that the organization needs more technicians. The evidence suggests capacity pressure is real, but avoidable repeat visits and late schedule changes are consuming capacity that should first be recovered through process, data, and governance improvements.

Quantified Impact of Status Quo

$2.3M estimated annual cost of inaction, based on $1.15M repeat-visit labor cost, $620K overtime and expedited parts cost, and $530K revenue leakage from missed same-week appointments.

Scope Definition

In scope
  • Two-region pilot
  • Dispatch readiness workflow
  • Parts availability checks
  • Supervisor exception queue
  • Operational metrics dashboard
  • Change-management and training plan
Out of scope
  • Full ERP replacement
  • Technician compensation redesign
  • National rollout before pilot exit criteria
  • Warehouse network redesign

Urgency Drivers

Peak service season begins in Q4. If the pilot does not start in July, the organization will enter peak season without validated routing and parts-readiness controls.

5. Recommended Solution

Recommendation

Approve Option 2: a two-region dispatch and parts readiness pilot with defined readiness gates, exception ownership, and operating metrics before any national rollout.

Rationale

Option 2 addresses the likely root cause while preserving reversibility. It is less expensive and faster than platform replacement, and it avoids adding permanent labor before recovering avoidable capacity leakage.

People / Process / Technology

People: Regional service directors sponsor the pilot; dispatch supervisors own daily exception review; inventory planning owns parts readiness; IT supports data extracts and dashboard enablement.

Process: Before appointments are locked, orders are checked for technician skill, parts availability, customer readiness, and schedule feasibility. Exceptions enter a supervisor-owned queue with escalation thresholds.

Technology: Use current work-order, inventory, and scheduling systems with a lightweight reporting layer and exception workflow for the pilot. Avoid major platform replacement in Phase 1.

Dependencies & Prerequisites

  • Access to work-order history
  • Inventory availability data extract
  • Regional supervisor participation
  • Pilot training calendar
  • CFO approval for Year 1 funding

Organizational Impact

The pilot changes how supervisors commit appointments and manage exceptions. It adds up-front readiness discipline but should reduce late-day rework, repeat visits, and overtime pressure.
