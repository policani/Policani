Case Study Markdown Builder

Turn one real work example into a clear story you can use for resumes, cover letters, and interviews. Pick one situation, explain what was wrong, what you did, and what got better.

1. Pick the Kind of Story

Choose the first option for most work stories: fixing a process, finishing a project, solving a problem, or getting messy work under control. Choose the second option when the story is mainly about money, budget, customers, risk, rules, audits, or a large amount of work.

Simple story patternWhat was happening? Why was it a problem? What did you do? What changed afterward?
Use numbers when you canNumbers can be dollars, weeks, hours, requests, tickets, customers, people, locations, mistakes, meetings, deadlines, or percent changes.
If you do not have a numberUse a clear before-and-after statement, such as faster responses, fewer repeats, clearer ownership, less rework, better reporting, or fewer escalations.

Plain example: Pick the first option if the story is about fixing a broken request process. Pick the second option if the story is mainly about saving money or reducing risk.

2. What This Story Helps Prove

Tell the system what kinds of jobs or skills this story supports. This helps it reuse the story in the right places later.

Plain example: This helps prove operations, project delivery, customer escalation, process improvement, and team coordination.

Plain example: In 2024, a customer operations team had more work requests than the old tracking process could handle.

3. The Problem and How Big It Was

Explain why the problem mattered. Add numbers where you can: how many people, requests, customers, dollars, systems, locations, or weeks were involved.

Plain example: 180 open requests were slowing down follow-up, three teams were using different trackers, and leaders needed a clear update by month-end.

Plain example: 180 requests, 3 teams, 2 customer groups, weekly leader review, and 1 shared dashboard.

Plain example: The obvious problem was a request backlog. The deeper problem was unclear ownership, inconsistent notes, and no shared priority rules.

4. Choices You Had

List the realistic choices. They do not have to be dramatic. This section simply shows that you thought through the problem before acting.

Plain example: Option 1 - keep the old tracker. Pros: quick. Cons: still messy. Option 2 - buy a new tool. Pros: powerful. Cons: too slow. Option 3 - standardize the tracker. Pros: fast and easy to adopt. Cons: required weekly follow-up.

Plain example: I chose a simple intake standard and dashboard because it fixed the visibility problem quickly without waiting for a new system.

5. What You Did

Say what you personally did. Also be clear about what you did not own, approve, or control.

Plain example: I coordinated the reset, wrote the rules, built the dashboard, and kept team leads aligned. I did not make final staffing decisions.

Plain example: Grouped requests by urgency, created a shared status view, set a weekly review, and documented the new intake rules.

6. What Changed

Write what got better. A measured result is best, but a clear before-and-after change is also useful.

Plain example: Reduced open requests from 180 to 42 in six weeks, gave leaders a weekly status view, and reduced duplicate follow-up messages.

Plain example: Before, requests lived in scattered notes. After, each request had an owner, priority, status, and next step.

Plain example: Customers got faster answers, and leaders could see where help was needed.

Plain example: Measured: backlog count from the weekly tracker. Observed: fewer escalation messages. Reasonable conclusion: planning improved because leaders used the same dashboard each week.

7. Short Version and Boundaries

Write the short version employers can understand. Then list what the story proves and what would be exaggerating.

Plain example: Led a backlog reset that clarified ownership, improved leader visibility, and reduced open requests from 180 to 42 in six weeks.

Plain example: This story proves backlog reduction, weekly operating rhythm, dashboard visibility, team coordination, and practical process documentation.

Plain example: Do not claim direct revenue impact, hiring authority, or ownership of the customer platform.
